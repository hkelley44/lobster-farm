# Eugene Schwartz — Frameworks

Eugene M. Schwartz wrote *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966) — arguably the most important direct-response copywriting book ever. He worked in the mail-order industry through its golden age. The book is out of print and rare hardcovers go for $400+. Public summaries are good; primary text is paywalled by scarcity. We work from public summaries and cite carefully.

## 1. The Five Stages of Awareness

**What it is.** Every prospect sits at one of five awareness levels regarding your product. Your copy must match their level. Mismatch = no conversion.

**The five stages.**
1. **Unaware** — they don't know they have the problem. Hardest to sell. Must lead with a story or shock that surfaces the problem.
2. **Problem-Aware** — they know they have the problem, don't know solutions exist. Lead with empathy for the pain. Introduce the category.
3. **Solution-Aware** — they know solutions exist, don't know yours specifically. Lead with mechanism — what makes yours different from category alternatives.
4. **Product-Aware** — they know your product, haven't bought. Lead with offer, proof, risk reversal.
5. **Most Aware** — they know and want your product. Lead with the deal. Price, discount, urgency.

**The implication.** Your headline should be calibrated to the awareness level of the traffic source.
- Cold paid traffic from a broad interest → mostly Stages 1-2.
- Retargeting traffic from your blog → Stages 2-3.
- Visitors from a "best UFC betting tools" review article → Stages 3-4.
- Existing email list → Stages 4-5.

**Applied — CombatCall.**
- Stage 1 hook ("you might not know this loses money"): "If you bet on UFC, you're probably leaking money to the closing line and don't know it."
- Stage 2 hook ("you know the problem"): "Sick of losing money chasing -350 favorites? There's a smarter way to read a card."
- Stage 3 hook ("you know solutions exist"): "Every betting model uses the same fight stats. Ours weights them for power-accuracy by camp. Here's what's different."
- Stage 4 hook ("you know us, haven't bought"): "Still on the fence? Try three fights free. No card. Walk away if it doesn't hit."
- Stage 5 hook ("you're ready"): "Annual subscriber price drops 33% on UFC 300 fight week. Tap in."

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 3. Public summary references in `sources.md`.

## 2. The Five Stages of Market Sophistication

**What it is.** As a market matures, the same claim loses power. Sophistication is a property of the MARKET, not the prospect. Each stage demands a different copy strategy.

**The five stages.**

1. **Stage 1 — First to market.** Make the simplest possible direct claim. ("This pill cures headaches.") Nobody has heard the claim before, so it lands.

2. **Stage 2 — Competitors arrive.** Make a bigger version of the same claim. ("This pill cures headaches in 10 minutes.") Adding superlatives and specifics.

3. **Stage 3 — Claims max out.** The market stops believing claims because they've all been made. Now you must introduce a UNIQUE MECHANISM. ("This pill cures headaches because it contains the patented X compound that targets the Y receptor.") Mechanism = the WHY.

4. **Stage 4 — Mechanisms multiply.** Competitors all have mechanisms now. You need a BIGGER mechanism. ("This pill cures headaches with our 7-stage micro-pellet delivery system that releases the X compound in pulses.") More elaborate, more "why."

5. **Stage 5 — Saturation.** Nobody believes anything. Strategies that work:
   - Identify with the prospect's experience (Schwartz: "You've tried everything. We get it.")
   - New audience (find an audience that hasn't seen the previous 4 stages yet)
   - New mechanism (start a new sub-category)
   - Movement / identity (Brunson territory)

**The key insight.** Most failed copy fails because the marketer is writing Stage 1 copy for a Stage 4 market. The audience has heard every promise. They don't want a bigger promise — they want a mechanism.

**Applied — CombatCall.** **MMA betting picks is Stage 4-5.**
- The market is saturated with tout services, Discord groups, "lock of the day" tweeters.
- Stage 1 copy ("we make money betting UFC") will be ignored.
- Stage 4 strategy: lead with the MECHANISM. "We score every fight on a power-accuracy-weighted +EV model trained on every UFC fight since 2010."
- Stage 5 strategy: identification. "We've all bought the pick service that disappeared after a bad month. We're not that."
- Combined approach (recommended): mechanism + identification. "You've been burned. So have we. Here's the actual model we built — and three picks free, every event, before you ever pay."

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 4.

## 3. Mass Desire

**What it is.** Schwartz's foundational claim: you cannot CREATE desire. You can only identify pre-existing mass desires and channel them through your product. Marketing is a parasite on desire that already exists.

**The corollary.** Stop trying to convince. Start trying to identify which existing desire your product satisfies, then point it at them like a gun.

**Applied — CombatCall.** The desires already exist in the MMA betting market:
- "Win more / lose less."
- "Stop feeling stupid after a bad night."
- "Be the one in the group chat who called it."
- "Have a real edge, not just a feeling."

You don't need to create these. You just need to claim them better than the competition.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 1.

## 4. Headline Verbalization (38 Techniques)

**What it is.** Schwartz's Ch. 4 catalogs 38 ways to "verbalize" the same core claim — same desire, different expression, sometimes 50x the pull. You don't write 38 different headlines; you take ONE core promise and run it through several techniques to find the strongest expression.

**A sample of techniques** (per public summaries — full list in the book):
- Restate the headline as a question.
- Quantify the result ("Lose 17 pounds in 6 weeks").
- Name the avatar ("For men over 40 who...").
- Add a "specific exception" ("...even if you've failed before").
- Time-bound it.
- Reverse it / make it counterintuitive.
- Pair the claim with proof ("...as proven by...").
- Use a number to chunk it ("3 reasons why...").
- Borrow authority ("Harvard study reveals...").
- Use the word "you" early and often.
- Open with a story stub ("How I lost...").
- Frame as discovery ("They laughed when I...").

**The principle.** Schwartz wrote: your headline's only job is to make the prospect read the SECOND sentence. The second sentence's only job is to make them read the third. Etc.

**Applied — CombatCall.** Take a single core claim ("CombatCall helps you bet UFC smarter") and verbalize it 10+ ways:
- "We analyzed every UFC fight since 2010. Here's what to bet Saturday."
- "How a broke-bettor's spreadsheet became a profitable UFC model."
- "If you bet UFC, three free picks every card. No card required."
- "The smartest UFC bettors don't trust their gut. They run the numbers."
- "Stop guessing at UFC cards. Start with the data."
- "For UFC bettors who'd rather know than feel."
- "73% of UFC betting is signal you can measure. 27% is noise. Here's the signal."

Then A/B test. The "right" headline is empirical.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 4. Full list lives in the book — see GAPS.md re: purchasing.

## 5. The Wheel of Persuasion (Less Famous, Worth Knowing)

**What it is.** Schwartz's claim that any prospect can be persuaded by hitting one of three things: their *self*, their *world*, or their *other people* (peer group/society).

- Self-focused: "What kind of person are you becoming?"
- World-focused: "What's happening out there that you need to react to?"
- Other-focused: "What will people think of you?"

CombatCall application: "be the smart one in the group chat" is Other-focused. "Stop letting Vegas take your money" is World-focused. "Become a data bettor" is Self-focused. Test which lands for which segment.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, scattered through the later chapters.

## 6. The Three Forces of Mass Desire (Measuring It)

Schwartz: marketing is a parasite on existing desire. But not all desires are equally exploitable. He proposes measuring any candidate desire on three dimensions before betting your campaign on it.

1. **Urgency / Intensity.** How sharp is the pain? Constant joint pain (high) vs. occasional mild headache (low). A toothache outsells a "want to be healthier" goal every day.
2. **Staying Power.** How resistant is the market to satiation? Hunger (high — recurs every 4 hours) vs. wanting a fancier car (low — one purchase satisfies for years). Recurring desire = recurring sales.
3. **Scope.** How many people share it? Loss aversion (everyone) > prevention-of-something-bad (some) > optimization-of-something-good (few).

**The rule.** Pick the desire where your product hits ALL THREE. If you can only hit two, hit Urgency + Scope.

**Applied — CombatCall.** The desire "stop bleeding money on UFC bets" hits all three:
- Urgency: high after every losing card.
- Staying power: card every Saturday — the desire never satiates.
- Scope: every UFC bettor.
Compare to "be smarter at bankroll management" — high scope, but low urgency until they lose. Lead with the bleed, not the optimization.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 1 — public synthesis at growthsummary.com, panictank, Aure's Notes (cited in sources.md).

## 7. The Forces That Shape Mass Desire

Schwartz splits the inputs to mass desire into two buckets — useful for ad creative when you're hunting for which lever to pull.

**Permanent forces** (always present, never go away):
- **Mass instinct** — beauty, safety, longevity, status, respect, sex, security.
- **Universal problems** — physical discomfort, money worry, social isolation, mortality.

**Forces of change** (rise and fall with time):
- **Trend cycles** — a desire moves through beginning → fulfillment → reversal. Catch a desire on the way up; abandon it on the way down.
- **Mass education** — markets get more sophisticated. Schwartz's awareness/sophistication frameworks are the OUTPUT of this force.

**Implication.** Permanent forces are safer bets for the spine of your offer. Forces of change are where breakout campaigns live — but they have a half-life.

**Applied — CombatCall.** The permanent force is "win money / avoid loss." The force of change is "the legalization of online sports betting in the US (2018+) and the rise of MMA betting as a category." That's a wave to surf — and to plan an exit if MMA betting ever consolidates into a few large platforms with proprietary tools.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 1-2.

## 8. The Three-Step Channeling Process (the actual workflow)

This is the chapter that operationalizes "channel, don't create."

1. **Select the most powerful applicable desire.** Score candidates on urgency × staying power × scope. Pick one.
2. **Acknowledge it in the headline.** Reinforce, intensify, and offer the solution in ONE statement. The headline's only job is to make them read the second sentence.
3. **Connect product to desire-satisfaction.** Every product has two elements: (a) the **physical product** (what it IS — features, ingredients, components) and (b) the **product in action** (what it DOES for the user — the lived outcome). The physical product DOESN'T SELL. Its four jobs are: (i) justify the price, (ii) prove the performance claim, (iii) assure continued performance, (iv) sharpen mental imagery of the benefit. The product-in-action sells.

**Applied — CombatCall.**
- Physical product: a SaaS app showing UFC fight cards with model-generated picks + stats.
- Product in action: "I check my phone Friday afternoon, see three +EV plays, drop $50 on each, wake up Sunday up $300." That's the lived outcome.
- Landing copy job: ground every feature claim in the lived outcome. NOT "ML-trained model with 73% accuracy on closing line" — INSTEAD "the difference between a Saturday you screenshot and a Saturday you delete the app."

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 2-3. Cross-referenced in Japkin (Medium) and growthsummary.

## 9. The Seven Techniques of Breakthrough Copy

This is the heart of the book and the part the first agent missed. Once you've selected the desire, acknowledged it in the headline, and identified the lived outcome, the BODY COPY uses some combination of these seven techniques. Tristan uses these like a chef uses spice cabinets — usually 3-4 per piece, layered.

**1. Intensification.** Amplify the desire by making the benefit vivid through repeated, varied expressions. 13 micro-methods including: show the claim in action (not stated, demonstrated), bring the reader INTO the scenario (second-person), stretch the benefit across time (10 years of compounded gain, not just today), validate via expert/peer, compare for superiority, show ease of achievement, use metaphor, leverage a guarantee. Intensification = volume knob for desire.

**2. Identification.** Build "saleable personality" into the product. The product carries an IDENTITY — character-based ("the kind of person who drinks this whiskey") and achievement-based ("the kind of person who owns this car"). Prospect projects desired self-image onto product. Brunson's Attractive Character + Hormozi's "you are X" are downstream of Identification.

**3. Gradualization.** When the claim is too big to swallow whole, build a chain of acceptances. Start with a statement no rational prospect can reject. Add the next-easiest. Continue until you've led them across a bridge of belief to your big claim. Classic Schwartz example: a TV-repair DIY guide tanked with the headline "Save $80/yr by repairing your own TV" — prospect didn't believe it. Reworked to "Why haven't TV owners been told these facts?" — opens with a question they can't disagree with, then walks them step-by-step. Massive winner.

**Applied — CombatCall.** Big claim: "you can profit from UFC betting consistently." Prospects don't believe it. Gradualize:
- "Most UFC bettors lose money." (Everyone agrees.)
- "Most bet on hype, gut, or the favorite-by-default." (Everyone agrees.)
- "Hype + gut + favorite-by-default is a losing strategy long-term." (Hard to argue.)
- "If you bet using data instead, your record would be different." (Probably true.)
- "We built the data." (Now they're at the offer.)

**4. Redefinition.** Reframe what an objection means. "Too expensive" becomes "an investment that pays back in 30 days." "Too complex" becomes "the system we built handles complexity FOR you — you just read the card." Three sub-methods: simplification (collapse complexity), escalation (the cost of inaction is bigger), price reduction (reframe to per-use cost).

**5. Mechanization.** Name and dramatize the WHY behind the product. This is what Hormozi calls "mechanism" and Schwartz invented. Don't just claim — explain the proprietary engine. "The 7-stage micro-pellet delivery system" beats "fast pain relief." Give the mechanism a NAME — "the Power-Accuracy Algorithm" beats "our model." A named mechanism is a moat.

**Applied — CombatCall.** Coin the mechanism. "The CombatCall Edge Score™" — composite signal trained on every UFC fight since 2010, weighted by recency, camp, weight cut, and closing-line behavior. The name does work. The fact that it has a name says "this is engineered, not vibes."

**6. Concentration.** Destroy the alternative satisfactions. The prospect could meet the desire through 5 other paths. Your copy systematically dismisses each. "Want to win UFC bets? You COULD trust Twitter cappers (got banned for past results), pay $99/mo tout services (no track record), bet only favorites (the closing line ate that arbitrage years ago), or use a sportsbook tipping service (they're paid by the book). None of those work. Here's why ours does."

**7. Camouflage.** Borrow conviction. Authority transfer. A doctor in a white coat. An MMA Hall of Famer endorsing. A Harvard study. A New York Times reference. The trick: the AUTHORITY doesn't have to be ABOUT the product — it just has to lend its credibility to the claim. "Endorsed by data scientist who built quantitative models for a top hedge fund" lends Wall Street credibility to a UFC betting tool.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, Ch. 6-9 (mapping approximate from public summaries: aslbk.com, spdrdng.com, valchanova.me — see sources.md).

## 10. The Five Final-Copy Touches

After the 7 techniques, Schwartz layers 5 final passes over the copy before shipping:

1. **Verification** — every claim backed by an authority, statistic, or live demonstration.
2. **Reinforcement** — does the headline claim reappear, in varied forms, throughout the body? (It should — at least 5x.)
3. **Interweaving** — does the copy alternate between emotion, image, and logic? Each one alone fails. The braid sells.
4. **Sensitivity** — does the copy address the reader where they are RIGHT NOW, in their literal current emotional state? (Tired? Skeptical? Burned before? Address it.)
5. **Momentum** — does each paragraph make the next one harder to NOT read? Schwartz's "the headline's job is to make them read the next sentence" rule applied recursively.

**Tristan's pre-ship checklist.** Run every long-form asset (sales page, email sequence, ad script) through this five-touch pass before pushing live.

**Source.** *Breakthrough Advertising*, final chapters.

## 11. The Complete 38(+2) Headline Verbalization Techniques

The first pass mentioned the 38 list exists in the book and named ~12 of them. Here's the full list synthesized from two careful third-party sources (Steven Abraham on Medium + Roy Furr at Breakthrough Marketing Secrets — both cited in sources.md). Each one is paired with a CombatCall verbalization to make it usable.

**Quantification family:**
1. **Measure the size of the claim** — `"20,000 filter traps"` → "We grade every single UFC matchup against 47 data points."
2. **Measure the speed of the claim** — `"Feel better FAST!"` → "Get Saturday's picks 48 hours before the bell."
3. **Compare the claim** — `"Six times whiter washes!"` → "73% more accurate than betting the favorite."

**Vividness family:**
4. **Metaphorize the claim** — `"Banishes corns!"` → "Reads UFC cards like a poker pro reads a table."
5. **Sensitize the claim** — `"Tastes like you just picked it"` → "The green-ledger Sunday morning you've been chasing."
6. **Demonstrate with a prime example** — `"Jake LaMotta fails to flatten Mono cup!"` → "Our model called the Nunes upset 14 days out. Here's the screenshot."

**Drama family:**
7. **Dramatize the claim or result** — `"They laughed when I sat down at the piano…"` → "They laughed when I told them I was using a spreadsheet to bet UFC. Now my bankroll is 4x."
8. **State as a paradox** — `"How a bald-headed barber saved my hair!"` → "How losing $3K on UFC made me a profitable bettor."
9. **Remove a limitation** — `"Shrinks hemorrhoids without surgery!"` → "Beat the closing line without a sharps account."

**Authority family:**
10. **Associate with aspirational figures** — `"Mickey Mantle says: Camels…"` → "Endorsed by [credentialed name when you have one]."
11. **Show detailed work / sub-benefits** — `"Relief from all 5 acid stomach troubles!"` → "5 stat models. 12 fighter dimensions. 1 pick per card."
12. **State as a question** — `"Who else wants a whiter wash?"` → "Who else is sick of getting cooked by underdogs?"
13. **Offer how-to information** — `"How to Win Friends and Influence People"` → "How to find +EV in any UFC card before fight night."
14. **Tie authority into the claim** — `"Boss mechanic shows how…"` → "Wall Street quant shows how he models UFC fight cards."
15. **Before-and-after** → "I used to buy picks from tipsters. I lost money. Now I run my own model. I'm up. The only thing that changed is I stopped trusting gut feel."

**Newness/exclusivity family:**
16. **Stress newness** — `"Announcing! Guided missile spark plugs!"` → "Introducing the first UFC betting tool trained on closing-line behavior."
17. **Stress exclusivity** — `"Ours alone! Persian Lamb originals"` → "The only model that weights for late weight-cut depletion."

**Challenge family:**
18. **Turn into a challenge** — `"Which twin has the Toni?"` → "Pick the winner of UFC 304 Co-Main. We bet you can't beat our model."
19. **Case-history quotation** — `"Look mom — no cavities!"` → "'Made back my year-long sub on UFC 300 alone.' — @bettor_handle"

**Compression family:**
20. **Condense the claim** — `"A ring and piston job in a tube!"` → "Your bankroll's edge, in one weekly subscription."
21. **Symbolize the claim** — `"The Atlantic Ocean becomes one-fifth as long!"` → "From 47% win rate to 64% — overnight."
22. **Connect mechanism to claim** — `"Floats fat right out of your body!"` → "Scores fights on the same axes the closing line uses — then beats it by 6%."
23. **Startle by contradicting expected mechanism** — `"Hit hell out of the ball with your right hand"` → "Stop reading fight breakdowns. They're poisoning your picks."

**Need framing family:**
24. **Connect need and claim** → "Lost a bet to a heavy favorite? You bet without data. Here's the data."
25. **Offer information in the ad itself** — `"Why men crack…"` → "Why 76% of UFC bettors lose money over a 12-month window."
26. **Turn into a case history** — `"Aunt Meg, who never married…"` → "Cole, the guy in the group chat who never had a system, until he did."
27. **Name the problem** — `"Day-time fatigue → Alka-Seltzer"` → "Hype-bet hangover. Every Sunday morning. Until you stop using vibes."
28. **Warn about pitfalls** — `"Don't invest one cent…"` → "Don't bet a dollar on UFC 305 until you read this."

**Style family:**
29. **Emphasize through phraseology** — `"Nobody but nobody undersells Gimbel's!"` → "We don't sell picks. We don't sell hype. We sell math."
30. **Show ease via universal limitation** — `"If you can count to 11…"` → "If you can read a fight card, you can run our system."
31. **State the difference** — `"The difference is in the additives!"` → "Every other UFC tool fits fights to bets. We fit bets to fights."
32. **Surprise — overcome limitation** — `"Crush a Hartman DC-8? Nothing!"` → "We called the Strickland-Adesanya upset when every model in our universe said no."
33. **Address people who can't buy** — `"If you've already taken your vacation, don't read this"` → "If you don't bet on UFC, this isn't for you."
34. **Address prospect directly** — `"To the man who will settle for nothing less than the presidency of his firm"` → "For UFC bettors who'd rather know than feel."
35. **Dramatize production difficulty** — `"When Jens finished designing this candleholder…"` → "We hand-tagged 2,847 UFC fights so the model could learn."
36. **Accuse of being too good** — `"Is it immoral to make money this easily?"` → "Should this even be public? (Our lawyers said it was fine.)"
37. **Challenge limiting beliefs** — `"You are twice as smart as you think"` → "You're a better bettor than your last 5 cards suggest."
38. **Question and answer** — `"You don't know what's under the hood…"` → "How does the model know? Here's the engine room, fully open."

**Bonus +2 (from the same compilations):**
39. **Feature the guarantee** — `"Don't pay a penny until…"` → "Three free picks every card. Walk away if they don't hit by Fight 3."
40. **If-then structure** — `"If you give me 20 minutes…"` → "Give us 90 days. We'll show you what data betting does to your year."

**How to use the 38.** You don't pick one. You take your CORE PROMISE (one sentence) and run it through 10-15 of these formulas in a rapid drafting session. Then A/B test the strongest 3-5. The "right" headline is empirical.

**Source.** Steven Abraham (Medium), Roy Furr (Breakthrough Marketing Secrets), and aslbk synthesis — all in sources.md. The list as transcribed in these public sources matches Schwartz's chapter 4 structure; specific phrasings are public-summary level (no chapter verbatim).

## 12. The Schwartz Working Method — 33:33 Sprints

Not a copywriting technique — a Schwartz operating practice with massive implications for how Tristan structures his own work.

**The system.** Schwartz worked in 33 minute, 33 second sprints. Timer on. During the sprint:
- Could not leave the desk.
- Could stare out the window, doodle, sip coffee.
- Could not do ANY task other than the copy in front of him.
- Could not check anything else.

Between sprints: 10-minute walk. Repeat.

**Why 33:33.** Arbitrary. Schwartz claimed any consistent short interval works; the specificity is what gives the brain permission to commit. The point is: long enough to build state, short enough that "I can suffer through this" is true.

**The result.** Schwartz wrote in approximately 3 hours of timed sprints per day, 5 days a week. He claimed an 85% hit rate (ads that profited). His annual output rivaled or exceeded copywriters working 12-hour days.

**Quote attributed to Schwartz:** *"When I press the start button, I can do anything I want. All willpower is dissolved. I can do anything as long as it relates to the piece of copy in front of me."*

**Applied — Tristan.** Use this for any deep-work asset: long-form sales pages, multi-email sequences, ad-creative brainstorms. Use the daily logs to track output per sprint over time. The constraint is the feature.

**Source.** Brian Kurtz commentary at briankurtz.net; Copywriter's Roundtable; Copyblogger summary; Ship30for30 — see sources.md.
