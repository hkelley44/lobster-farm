## 1. Dan Kennedy — *No B.S.* series, *Magnetic Marketing*

**Who.** The godfather of late-20th-century direct-response marketing. Hormozi, Sabri, and Brunson all cite Kennedy as direct lineage. Brunson co-wrote *Magnetic Story Selling* with him in 2024-25. Kennedy is who Brunson learned funnels from.

**Core frameworks.**
- **The Kennedy Results Triangle: Message + Market + Media.** Most marketers fail by getting one wrong. The RIGHT message to the WRONG market = no sale. The RIGHT message + market on the WRONG media = no sale. All three have to align.
- **"Match bait to critter."** Different audiences require different lead magnets. The bait that catches one prospect repels another.
- **Every communication must have an offer.** Most marketing fails by not asking for action. There's always an offer — even if the offer is "click this link."
- **The 10 No B.S. Rules of Direct Marketing:**
  1. Always include an offer.
  2. Give clear instructions for response.
  3. Track every dollar.
  4. Build brand as a BYPRODUCT, not the goal.
  5. Multi-step follow-up systems.
  6. Write to the psyche, not the product.
  7. Write emotionally and conversationally.
  8. Make huge claims; be hyperbolic.
  9. Always have a clear, measurable response mechanism.
  10. Maintain marketing discipline.

**Famous quotes:**
> *"Results rule. Period."*
> *"Timid salesmen have skinny kids."*

**When to use Kennedy.** Whenever Tristan needs to defend a "loud" creative choice against an instinct to be tasteful. Kennedy's posture: tasteful doesn't sell.

**Source.** *No B.S. Direct Marketing*, *Magnetic Marketing*, *Magnetic Story Selling* (with Brunson). magneticmarketing.com.

## 2. Gary Halbert — *The Boron Letters*

**Who.** Legendary direct-mail copywriter, mentor to Brunson (early), influence on Schwartz's modern interpreters. *The Boron Letters* is 25 letters he wrote to his son from prison (Boron Federal Prison Camp). The most-quoted modern direct-response text after Schwartz.

**Core frameworks.**
- **The Starving Crowd.** Halbert's most-famous metaphor: if you owned a hamburger stand, what's the one thing you'd want? Not the best burger, not the best recipe — A STARVING CROWD. Find hungry markets first; product second.
- **The 40/40/20 Rule.** 40% of campaign success = the offer. 40% = the audience (right market). 20% = the copy. Most copywriters obsess over the smallest variable.
- **The AIDA spine** (refined): Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. Halbert added: editorial-style copy out-performs ad-style copy 5:1 (he called it the "advertorial").
- **The Dollar Bill Letter** — eight-page direct-mail piece with a real $1 attached at the top. Triggers reciprocity (Cialdini), pattern interrupt, and unmissable salience.
- **Hand-copying.** Halbert's discipline: hand-copy 1 great sales letter PER DAY. Builds muscle memory for what works.

**Famous quotes:**
> *"The money is where the enthusiasm is."*
> *"If you put your mind to work, you won't have to use cheap shots."*
> *"Motion over meditation."*

**When to use Halbert.** When Tristan needs to bias toward MARKET selection over copy obsession. When stuck on copy, switch to picking a better market.

**Source.** *The Boron Letters* (Halbert's son made the letters public for free — find at thegaryhalbertletter.com).

## 3. Claude Hopkins — *Scientific Advertising* (1923, PUBLIC DOMAIN)

**Who.** Early-20th-century mail-order and packaged-goods copywriter. Brand campaigns: Pepsodent, Schlitz Beer, Palmolive, Quaker Oats, Puffed Wheat. *Scientific Advertising* (1923) is the foundational text. **Public domain — read free at archive.org and at scientificadvertising.com.**

**Core frameworks.**
- **"Advertising is salesmanship."** Treat every ad as a salesperson talking to ONE prospect. If a salesperson wouldn't say it face-to-face, cut it.
- **TEST EVERYTHING.** Hopkins invented direct-response testing as a discipline. Small test → measure → scale winners → kill losers. The ad business is a science, not an art.
- **The more you tell, the more you sell.** Long copy outperforms short copy when the prospect is interested. The job is to interest them; once interested, deliver the full case.
- **Specificity beats vagueness.** "Used in 52 nations" > "used worldwide." "78-second shave" > "fast shave." Numbers create credibility.
- **FREE SAMPLES.** Hopkins pioneered the free-sample-via-coupon — the original lead magnet. Prospect requests the sample = self-qualified.

**Famous quotes:**
> *"Do nothing to merely interest, amuse, or attract."*
> *"You don't know anything until you test."*
> *"The more you tell, the more you sell."*

**Famous campaigns.**
- **Pepsodent** — "Removes the film — that's the secret of a beautiful smile." A mechanism positioning before mechanism was even named.
- **Schlitz Beer** — became #1 brand by being the first to describe brewing process in ads. Hopkins' insight: ALL beer was made this way, but no one was TELLING anyone.
- **Puffed Wheat** — "Food shot from guns!" Classic dramatization of the production process.

**When to use Hopkins.** When Tristan or Cole gets clever, distracted, or "creative." Hopkins is the corrective: cut everything that doesn't sell.

**Source.** *Scientific Advertising* (1923), public domain. archive.org/details/scientificadvert0000hopk. scientificadvertising.com hosts the full PDF.

## 4. David Ogilvy — *Confessions of an Advertising Man*

**Who.** "The Father of Advertising." Founded Ogilvy & Mather in 1948. Famously bridged Madison Avenue brand-advertising with direct-response discipline — he respected both. *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963) sold 1M+ copies and is the brand-side bible.

**Core frameworks.**
- **"No great campaign is possible without a Big Idea."** Ogilvy's central thesis: tactics are nothing without a memorable, ownable, scalable BIG IDEA.
- **Headlines do 80% of the work.** "On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent 80 cents out of your dollar."
- **Demonstrate the benefit specifically, factually.** Ogilvy hated vague brand puffery. Show the benefit, with specifics. Use the brand name in the headline.
- **Long copy works — IF written well.** Famously authored long-form ads (the Rolls-Royce ad ran 719 words).
- **Test everything.** A bridge between Hopkins and modern direct response. Ogilvy ran his agency as a test-driven shop.

**Famous campaigns / headlines.**
- **Rolls-Royce:** *"At sixty miles an hour, the loudest noise in the new Rolls-Royce comes from the electric clock."* Specificity. Implicit luxury. No adjectives needed.
- **Schweppes** — Commander Whitehead character — Brunson's "Attractive Character" before Brunson named it.
- **Dove** — positioned as "one-quarter cleansing cream" — mechanism positioning (Schwartz Stage 3).

**Famous quotes:**
> *"There is no substitute for homework. The more you know about a product, the more likely you are to come up with a big idea for selling it."*
> *"Tell the truth, but make the truth fascinating."*
> *"If you want to be interesting, be interested."*
> *"On the average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent 80 cents out of your dollar."*

**When to use Ogilvy.** When Tristan needs to defend BRAND-LEVEL thinking (mosaic, big idea, ownable concept) against pure-direct-response urgency. Ogilvy is the corrective in the OTHER direction from Hopkins.

**Source.** *Confessions of an Advertising Man* (1963, paid). Public summaries: cultmethod.com/articles/confessions-of-an-advertising-man/. Wikipedia: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confessions_of_an_Advertising_Man.

## How these four overlay on Tristan's brain

| Thinker | Posture | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **Kennedy** | Maximally direct-response, unapologetic | Defending a "loud" creative choice against a tasteful instinct |
| **Halbert** | Market > copy, hand-on, blue-collar | When stuck on copy — fix the market first |
| **Hopkins** | Scientific, testing-first, no clever | When the team is getting cute / distracted |
| **Ogilvy** | Big-idea, brand-aware, factual | When Tristan needs to defend a brand-mosaic move against short-term DR pressure |

The six primary lineages teach Tristan WHAT to do. These four adjacents teach Tristan POSTURE — when to push hard direct response, when to invest in brand, when to test, when to ship. Use them as compass calibration, not as primary playbook.
