# Pre-Suasion Tactics — Cross-Author Synthesis

Pre-suasion = the moment BEFORE your ask. Cialdini named it in 2016, but Schwartz, Hopkins, and Halbert had been using the same lever for decades. This file is the problem-shaped index: when Tristan needs to design the moment-before-the-ask, start here.

## The core insight (Cialdini's version)

> "Optimal persuasion is achieved only through optimal pre-suasion."

What captures the prospect's attention BEFORE you deliver the message determines whether the message lands. By focusing attention on a specific concept first, you can shift the answer to your ask without changing the prospect's underlying attitudes.

## The Privileged Moment

The window after a pre-suasive opener but before the ask. Brief. Powerful. Must be IMMEDIATELY followed by the ask (or a behavioral commitment) — privileged moments fade in seconds.

## The 8 Pre-Suasion Patterns (Tristan's working set)

### Pattern 1: Identity Activation
Ask a pre-question that activates the self-concept consistent with your desired behavior.
- "Do you consider yourself a helpful person?" → 29% to 77% compliance.
- CombatCall: "Are you serious about your UFC bankroll?" → THEN ask for the opt-in.
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5a.

### Pattern 2: Environmental Priming
Change the background. Change the buying criterion.
- Fluffy clouds → comfort-prioritized buyers. Coins → cost-prioritized buyers.
- CombatCall: green +EV scoreboard backgrounds on landing pages → primes "edge" as the buying criterion.
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5b.

### Pattern 3: Anchoring
Set a reference point — relevant or irrelevant — before the price reveal.
- Cialdini's friend: "I'm not gonna charge you a million dollars" → real quote felt small.
- CombatCall: show competitor pricing ($500-2000/mo tout services) BEFORE revealing $120/yr.
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5c. Cross-ref: `frameworks/offer-construction.md` on stacking-then-price.

### Pattern 4: Open Loops (Zeigarnik)
Leave it unfinished. The brain returns to complete it.
- Soft drink ads stopped 4-5 sec early outperformed complete ones on recall.
- CombatCall: subject lines like "What the model said about Pereira-Adesanya 3 (and why I almost ignored it)."
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5d. Cross-ref: `frameworks/copy-fundamentals.md` on curiosity hooks.

### Pattern 5: Social Proof — Match the Group
Closer the comparison group, more powerful the proof.
- "75% of guests in THIS ROOM reused towels" > "75% of guests" > "help the environment."
- CombatCall: "Subscribers WHO LOST $1K+ ON UFC LAST YEAR" > generic "subscribers."
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5g. Cross-ref: `frameworks/persuasion-principles.md`.

### Pattern 6: The Right Word (Metaphor Choice)
One word changes everything.
- "Crime is a beast" → catch-and-cage solutions. "Crime is a virus" → root-cause solutions.
- "Advice" (partner) vs "Opinion" (critic).
- CombatCall: book = "vampire" (weapon-seeking) vs "casino" (guide-seeking) vs "rule-book-less game" (curriculum-seeking). Test which lands.
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` sections 5h and 5k.

### Pattern 7: Gradualization (Schwartz's name for it)
A chain of acceptances. Start with something they can't disagree with. Build to the big claim.
- TV repair ad: "Save $80/year on TV repair" (didn't believe it) → "Why haven't TV owners been told these facts?" (couldn't disagree).
- CombatCall: "Most UFC bettors lose money" → "Most bet on hype" → "Hype + favorites is a losing strategy" → "If you bet from data, your record changes" → "We built the data."
- See: `authors/schwartz/frameworks.md` section 9, sub-technique 3 (Gradualization).

### Pattern 8: Post-Suasion Lock-In
The privileged moment fades in seconds. Immediately convert to a COMMITMENT (active, voluntary, effortful).
- McCain campaign: flag-prime → online registration → preference held 8+ months.
- CombatCall: every pre-suasive open → email opt-in OR click-through OR free-3-fights signup. Don't waste the moment.
- See: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` section 5l. Cross-ref: `authors/cialdini/frameworks.md` Principle #2 (Commitment & Consistency).

## The 4 Anti-Patterns (Never Do These)

### Anti-Pattern 1: Negative social proof
Petrified Forest sign showed three thieves → theft TRIPLED. You can't shame a behavior by saying it's common. You normalize it.
- NEVER: "Most UFC bettors lose money following Twitter cappers" as a leading anchor. You've just normalized "follow Twitter cappers."
- INSTEAD: "Our 1,200+ subscribers use the model on every card" — normalizes the desired behavior.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Open the loop, don't close it
Pre-suasion that opens curiosity but provides no payoff destroys trust.
- Clickbait headline → mismatched body → unsubscribe.
- Earn the curiosity, deliver on it.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Manufactured scarcity
Cialdini's "Sleuth/Smuggler/Bungler" framework: a fake countdown timer is the Smuggler move. Smugglers win once, lose the list forever.
- Real scarcity only. Fight-night windows are real. Founding member tiers are real (if finite). 24-hour discounts are real (if you actually shut them off).

### Anti-Pattern 4: Pre-suade and then forget the ask
The privileged moment lasts seconds. If your pre-suasion opens the door and your ask comes 3 paragraphs later, the moment is gone.
- Open the loop → answer with the ask immediately.
- Identity activation → commitment ask immediately.
- Anchor → price reveal immediately.

## Tristan's pre-flight checklist (run before any high-stakes asset)

For any landing page, email, sales asset, ad creative:

1. [ ] **What's the pre-suasive opener?** (Is there one? If not, write it.)
2. [ ] **What identity does it activate?** (Does the activation match the desired behavior?)
3. [ ] **What's the environmental priming?** (Background image / surrounding context — what's it voting for?)
4. [ ] **Is there an anchor?** (Reference price, reference behavior, reference identity — set BEFORE the ask.)
5. [ ] **Is there an open loop?** (Curiosity created — and resolved later?)
6. [ ] **Is the social proof the closest possible match group?** (Not generic "users" — same-situation users.)
7. [ ] **Have you chosen the right metaphor?** (One word can flip the response.)
8. [ ] **If the claim is big, do you gradualize?** (Bridge of belief from agreed-with to claimed.)
9. [ ] **Does the ask immediately follow the privileged moment?** (Within seconds, not paragraphs.)
10. [ ] **Is there a behavioral commitment at the close?** (Or does the moment evaporate?)

7+/10 = ship. <7/10 = redesign the moment-before-the-ask.
